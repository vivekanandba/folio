# Wealth creators — spotting tomorrow's compounders

> Yesterday's minnows became today's giants — but they looked ordinary, even expensive, at the start.

> [!key] A wealth creator is a business that **compounds earnings for years**, not a stock with a good story. The signal isn't a soaring price — it's rising return on capital, earnings that actually grow, and management that reinvests into widening the moat.

## Big idea

At the end of FY01, **Eicher Motors** was a ₹46 crore company, **Titan** ₹176 crore, **Marico** ₹41 crore. Two decades later they anchor serious long-term portfolios. Read those numbers slowly: the giants of today were unremarkable then. The question is what separated them from the thousands of small companies that stayed small — or blew up.

## The moat J-curve

Before a company earns extraordinary returns, it usually pays to **build** something — a distribution network, a process, a brand, a platform. This phase looks expensive: return on capital can actually *dip*, and impatient investors leave at exactly the wrong moment. Then a shift: pricing power, switching costs, or brand trust hold — and **return on capital rises**. That's the first hard financial evidence a moat is forming.

## Earnings are the only growth that counts

Peter Lynch's line, tested on Indian data: companies with steady **earnings growth** — Ajanta Pharma, Maruti Suzuki, Motherson — delivered returns that tracked those earnings almost exactly. The inverse held just as tightly: as Reliance Naval's debt ballooned and servicing it overwhelmed earnings, return on capital turned negative and the stock followed.

> [!warn] A rising price without rising earnings is a story, not a compounder. Price can run ahead of the business; over years, earnings pull it back to reality.

## Capital allocation is the differentiator

The companies that complete the journey have leadership that **reinvests returns into deepening the advantage** — not distributing prematurely, not diversifying into unrelated businesses. Deciding where each rupee goes, the unglamorous work of capital allocation, is what separates the ones that reach the next stage.

## Runway

A moat needs a large, still-unorganised market to grow into — the **runway**. "Digital India," for instance, is no longer about adding users; it's about more of household spending, borrowing and investing moving through organised channels. Big opportunity — but growth alone doesn't create wealth; it has to convert into durable earnings.

> [!more] The danger changes with size
> At the **small-cap** stage the risks are governance, leverage, poor disclosure, and fragile business models. At the **large-cap** stage the risk flips to **complacency** — moats that stop widening while challengers chip away. The sweet spot this framework hunts is a company *past* the fragile stage and showing real signs of a widening moat.

## Investor move

- Track **earnings and return on capital**, not the price chart.
- Look for the **ROCE inflection** after a heavy build phase — the moat forming.
- Check **where profits go**: reinvested into the core, or scattered?
- Demand a **runway**: is the addressable market big and still being organised?
