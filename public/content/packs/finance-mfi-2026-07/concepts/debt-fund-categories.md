# Debt fund categories & risk

> "Debt" is not one thing. The label hides two very different dials: how long, and how safe.

> [!key] Every debt fund sits on two axes — **duration** (how sensitive it is to interest-rate moves) and **credit** (how likely its borrowers are to repay). "Debt = safe" is wrong; a long-duration or low-credit fund can lose money.

## Big idea

Debt funds lend money and earn interest. Their risk comes from two independent choices the manager makes:

- **Duration risk** — longer average maturity ⇒ NAV swings more when rates change (rates up → prices down).
- **Credit risk** — lending to weaker borrowers pays more yield but risks default.

A SEBI category name mostly pins down the *duration* box; you still have to read the portfolio for *credit*.

## Matching fund to job

| Horizon / job | Sensible category |
|---|---|
| Cash for days–weeks | Liquid / overnight |
| 6–18 months, low drama | Ultra-short / money market |
| 2–3 years, some yield | Corporate bond / Banking & PSU |
| Rate view, long horizon | Gilt (accept duration swings) |

> [!warn] A gilt fund has almost no *credit* risk yet can still fall meaningfully when rates rise — that's **duration** risk. Two "safe-sounding" funds can behave nothing alike.

> [!more] The two-axis mental check
> Before buying any debt fund, answer both: (1) *How long is the money committed* → pick duration to match your horizon. (2) *Whose promises am I holding* → scan the credit-quality mix. Category name answers (1); the factsheet answers (2).

## Investor move

- Match **duration to your horizon**, not to the highest yield.
- Read the **credit-quality** breakdown — don't assume "debt" means "safe."
- Use liquid/ultra-short for the **Liquidity** pillar of your [plan](#/pack/finance-mfi-2026-07/concept/four-pillars-planning); take duration/credit risk only with eyes open.
