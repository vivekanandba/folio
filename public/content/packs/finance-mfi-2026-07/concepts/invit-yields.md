# InvIT yields — headline vs pocket

> A startling yield is a clue to investigate structure, not a reason to skip reading.

> [!key] The advertised **distribution yield** is not the **income** you keep. Part of every payout can be your own capital handed back, and the units can fall in price. Judge total return after structure, not the headline number.

## Big idea

Infrastructure Investment Trusts (InvITs) own income-producing infrastructure and pass cash to unitholders. They can advertise eye-catching annual yields — but **headline yield and money that actually reaches your pocket are different**, because of payout composition (income vs return of capital), fees, ageing assets, and unit-price movement.

## What you are buying

Buying an InvIT is really three decisions:

1. **Asset class** — what kind of infrastructure?
2. **Counterparty** — who pays, and how creditworthy are they?
3. **Concession profile** — the terms and remaining duration.

> [!more] Illustrative buckets (India listed space)
> - **Transmission** — long, regulated tariffs (steadier).
> - **Roads** — tolls, or hybrid-annuity contracts (traffic and policy risk).
> - **Gas pipelines** — tariffs vs a small set of shippers (concentration risk).

## Why yields mislead

> [!warn] A distribution is not a coupon. Assets and contracts age, cash flows are **not** a perpetual FD, and total return = distributions **plus** unit-price change — which can be negative.

## Investor move

Separate "looks like high income" marketing from "what I keep after structure and risk." Ask what fraction of the payout is genuine income, how long the concessions run, and how the units have moved.
