# SIP vs lumpsum & rupee-cost averaging

> A SIP is a discipline for *behaviour and cash flow* — not a mathematical guarantee of higher returns.

> [!key] **SIP** (investing a fixed sum monthly) and **lumpsum** (investing it all at once) aren't rivals — they answer different questions. If you already hold the cash, the maths usually favours investing it sooner; a SIP wins on discipline and on not needing the cash up front.

## Big idea

**Rupee-cost averaging** means your fixed monthly amount buys more units when prices are low and fewer when high, smoothing your average cost. It reduces the risk of investing everything at a peak — but because markets rise more often than they fall, a lumpsum invested early is *on average* exposed to more growth time.

## Which, when?

| Situation | Usually better | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly salary, no big pile of cash | **SIP** | Matches cash flow; builds the habit |
| You just received a large amount | **Lumpsum (or staggered)** | More time in market; avoids "waiting for a dip" |
| You're nervous about timing a peak | **Staggered / STP** | Splits a lumpsum over a few months to soften regret |

> [!warn] "SIP always beats lumpsum" is a myth. In steadily rising markets, delaying money via a SIP *lowers* the outcome versus investing it all early. The SIP's real edge is behavioural and cash-flow — not a return premium.

> [!more] What an STP is
> A **Systematic Transfer Plan** parks a lumpsum in a low-risk (usually liquid/debt) fund and moves a fixed slice into equity each month — a lumpsum you *feel* like a SIP. It's the usual compromise when you have a large sum but fear buying at a top.

## Investor move

- Don't hold cash "waiting for a correction" — decide SIP vs staggered and start.
- Use a SIP to automate the habit; use an STP to phase in a windfall.
- Judge the plan by whether you'll *stick to it*, not by back-tested perfection.
