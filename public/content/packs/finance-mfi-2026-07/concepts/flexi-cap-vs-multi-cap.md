# Flexi-cap vs multi-cap

> Category names sell a promise. Rules decide what managers *must* do. Freedom decides what they *can* ignore.

> [!key] **Multi-cap** funds are *forced* to hold ≥25% each in large, mid, and small caps. **Flexi-cap** funds only need ≥65% equity and can sit wherever they like — which often means large-cap-heavy. Same-sounding names, very different rulebooks.

## Big idea

Both are diversified equity categories, but built under different SEBI rules. Multi-caps must keep floors across all three market-cap buckets. Flexi-caps only need a high equity floor and may roam the capitalisation spectrum — so a flexi-cap can quietly behave like a large-cap fund.

## Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Multi-cap | Diversified equity, **minimum 25% each** in large / mid / small (post-2020 redesign) |
| Flexi-cap | Diversified equity, **≥65% equity**; no mandated mid/small floors |
| Style | Where the portfolio *actually* sits vs what the label suggests |

## What a "go-anywhere" fund can actually look like

A flexi-cap that has settled into large caps:

A true multi-cap cannot look like that: it must hold at least 25% mid and 25% small.

## Why it matters

If you buy "go anywhere" and get "mostly large caps forever," you may be paying **active fees** for something close to a large-cap fund — with a different return path than genuine all-cap exposure.

> [!more] Why the two categories exist
> Multi-caps were criticised for drifting into large caps. SEBI responded by mandating the three 25% floors. Flexi-cap was then created as the *unconstrained* alternative — legitimising go-anywhere mandates without floors. Read [style drift & the flexi-cap tax](#/pack/finance-mfi-2026-07/concept/style-drift-flexi-tax) for what happens next.

## Remember

- **Label** = marketing + regulatory bucket.
- **Holdings** = what you actually own.
- Check the large/mid/small mix periodically, not just the category name.
